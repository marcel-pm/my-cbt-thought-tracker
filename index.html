How CBT works

The core message: what you think and do affects how you feel. Our thinking can be biased, and different biases lead to different emotional problems. The goal is not to "think happy thoughts" — it is to think accurately.

Thoughts are not facts. Automatic thoughts pop into your mind, feel very believable, but can be inaccurate. The situation does not cause the feeling — your interpretation of it does. Epictetus put it this way nearly 2000 years ago: people are disturbed not by things, but by the notions they form about things.

Common thinking biases
Catastrophizing Seeing the worst in situations — leads to anxiety
Excluding the positive Discounting or dismissing good things — leads to low mood
Personalization Automatically blaming yourself for everything — leads to sadness and anxiety
Exercise instructions
Stage 1 — Thought log▼

When you notice a shift in how you feel, ask yourself: What was going through my mind just then? Write it down as quickly as possible. Many people find it powerful to see their thoughts recorded.

Aim for a few entries per day at times when you notice obvious emotional changes — not every thought.

1
Situation — Date and time, where you were, who you were with, what was happening just before the emotional shift.
2
Emotion and body — Describe the feeling in one word (Anxious, Sad, Angry). Rate intensity 0–100%. Note any physical sensations like tight chest or butterflies.
3
Thought — What were you saying to yourself? Any images or memories? Rate how much you believed it 0–100%.
Exercise 1 — Perspective-taking▼

Look at a situation that upset you from different perspectives. This is not about dismissing your feelings — it is about checking whether your interpretation is the only valid one.

1
Record the situation — What happened, who was there, when.
2
Record how you felt — Emotion and intensity 0–100%.
3
Your interpretation — How did you interpret what happened?
4
Consider other perspectives using these prompts:
  • What is another way of looking at this situation?
  • What would a trusted friend say to me?
  • Name 3 good outcomes that might come from this
  • Will this matter to me when I'm 80?
  • How would I respond to this with no fear?
  • What would someone who truly liked themselves think here?
5
Reflect — How do you feel now compared to when you started?
Exercise 2 — Thought on trial▼

Hold an imaginary court case. Your thought is in the dock. Your job is to be the defense, the prosecution, and the judge. Read everything out loud if you can — many people find this creates helpful distance from the thought.

1
Put the thought in the dock — State it clearly. Turn questions into statements: "Why am I so stupid?" becomes "I am so stupid."
2
Defense — What evidence supports this thought? Don't censor anything. Ask: "What makes me think this is true?"
3
Prosecution — What evidence suggests this thought is not 100% true all the time? Ask yourself: If a friend had this thought, what would I say? When was this thought last untrue? Have there ever been exceptions?
4
Verdict — Given all the evidence, what is the fairest, most balanced summary? Write your new thought. Two standards used in courts: "beyond reasonable doubt" or "on the balance of probabilities."
5
Reflect — What do you notice about your thoughts and emotions now?
Exercise 3 — Examining evidence (7-column)▼

The traditional CBT method for testing whether a thought is accurate. Similar to the court trial but uses different language. Good to use once you are comfortable with the basics.

1
Situation — Date, time, where, who, what was happening.
2
Emotion — Describe and rate intensity 0–100%.
3
Automatic thought — State it. Rate how much you believe it 0–100%.
4
Evidence for — All reasons the thought might be true.
5
Evidence against — All reasons it might not be true all the time.
6
Alternative thought — A balanced new thought that accounts for both sides. Rate how much you believe it.
7
Re-rate emotion — How do you feel now? What do you notice?
